There is no shortage of tools that can tell you when you blundered. They can show the evaluation drop, the tactic you missed, and the engine line that would have saved the position. That is useful. It has made chess analysis faster and more accessible for almost everyone.

But it still leaves a harder question unanswered: what was the position actually asking for?

For many players, that is the real barrier to improvement. They do not only want to know that a move was inaccurate. They want to understand why one plan made sense, why another plan did not, and what the game was really about.

## The gap we kept seeing

Most post-game tools are very good at telling you **when** the evaluation changed. They are much less good at telling you **why the position was leaning toward one plan and against another**.

That gap matters because a lot of practical chess is not about spotting a single tactic out of nowhere. It is about understanding what kind of game the position wants.

> The question we cared about was not only what the best move was, but what the position meant.

## The hard part of chess

The hardest mistakes in chess are not always tactical. Often, they are strategic.

A piece ends up on the wrong square. The wrong trade is made. A pawn break is delayed too long, or played before the position is ready. A player chooses action on the wrong side of the board and spends the next ten moves living with that decision.

Those mistakes do not always look dramatic in one moment. They show up as drift. They quietly shape the next phase of the game. And they are exactly the kind of mistakes that are hardest to explain with a single engine line.

## What we want Chesstory to do

We built Chesstory because we think players need more than a verdict. They need better explanations of plans, imbalances, pawn structure, piece placement, pressure points, and timing.

Not just where the evaluation changed, but why the position was pulling the game in one direction and resisting another.

That is why strategy sits at the center of the product. Tactical mistakes still matter. Calculation still matters. But strategy is often the part that tells you what to calculate for, what to improve first, and what kind of game you should be trying to play.

## What this journal is for

This space is where we will write down the why behind the product, the changes we ship, and what we are learning while building analysis around strategy.

Some posts will be product updates. Some will be design notes. Some will be about how we think chess explanations should work.

The goal is simple, even if the problem is not: make strategy easier to see.
