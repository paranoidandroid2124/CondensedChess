When we think about the long-term goal of Chesstory, the goal is not simply to generate more analysis.

The goal is to help players understand chess more clearly.

That means aiming beyond verdicts, beyond engine lines, and beyond post-game summaries that only tell you where the evaluation changed.

> If engines tell you what was best, we want to help players understand what the position wanted, why they missed it, and how to recognize it next time.

That is the direction we care about.

## Not just what happened, but what the position wanted

Most analysis tools are good at telling you that a move was inaccurate.

Far fewer are good at telling you what the position was actually asking for.

Was this a position about patience or acceleration? About improving the worst piece or forcing open the center? About preserving tension or simplifying into a structure that favored one side?

Those are strategic questions. They shape practical decisions long before a tactic appears on the board.

Our long-term goal is to make those questions easier to see.

## From one game to recurring patterns

A useful explanation should not disappear when the game ends.

Over time, we want Chesstory to become better at recognizing repeated strategic mistakes across many games.

Not just that a player missed a move, but that they repeatedly rush a pawn break, drift in positions that require restraint, trade the wrong defender, ignore the wrong file, or lose track of what kind of endgame they are steering toward.

That is where analysis starts becoming personal.

## From explanation to recognition

Good analysis is not only about describing the past well.

It should make the future easier to play.

The point is not simply to say, "Here is what you should have done."

The point is to make a player more likely to recognize the same strategic signal next time it appears in a different position, under practical conditions, without needing an engine to point it out first.

That is a much harder goal than post-game evaluation, but it is also the one that matters more.

## The real ambition

The real ambition is not to build a louder analysis tool.

It is to build something closer to a personal strategic coach for chess.

A system that helps players see plans more clearly, understand their own recurring blind spots, and carry lessons from one game into the next.

That does not mean pretending strategy can be reduced to a single formula. It means trying to make one of the hardest parts of chess more legible, more personal, and more trainable.

## What we are building toward

If the first stage of chess software was about finding the best move, one future stage should be about helping people understand the position itself.

What each side is playing for. Which imbalances matter. Which plans are natural. Which strategic errors keep returning. And how a player can start seeing them earlier.

That is what we are building toward.
